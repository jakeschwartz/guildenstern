The user will course-correct mid-build when an abstraction over-rotates. The corrections are usually small phrases that contain large structural information. Examples from day one:

- **"Color is less about names — a lot about agent/non agent, must respond, etc."** I had been on per-person identity colors; he flipped me to state-carrying. Reshaped the entire visual grammar.
- **"You're not going to talk through positions, you're going to vent and the agent mediates."** I had built arbitration as if both parties arrive with clean positions. He pointed out positions are *outputs* of a coaching process, not inputs. Reshaped the entire arbitration design.
- **"Agent is 90% of the content — with participants agreeing or not. Think couples counseling."** Sharpened the previous correction into a concrete UX implication: no composer on the shared surface, reactions only.

**Why:** He has a clearer model of the product than the design language I'm reaching for at any moment. His structural instincts are usually right; my abstractions are reasonable but often miss the lived behavior. He'll fix this in seconds when he sees the artifact.

**How to apply:**
- When he reframes a design, do not defend the previous version. The previous version was a stepping stone. Take the reframing seriously and rebuild.
- Surface his reframing back to him clearly so he knows it landed ("you're right — I over-rotated to X").
- After rebuilding, name the structural change cleanly so we both know what changed and why. Don't gloss.
- Trust that his "tabling" instinct is real — when he says "table that for later thought" he means it. Don't re-raise.
